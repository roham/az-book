Part VIII subjects the framework to its own retrospective acid test. Appendix E catalogs remaining gaps. This is version 2.0.

Prologue: Where This Process Comes From

Before this document tells you how to build, it owes you an answer to a simpler question: why should you trust it?

This innovation framework was extracted, under pressure, from a specific sequence of companies, products, and bets over more than a decade. The failures were expensive. The wins were unlikely. The process survived both.

Axiom Zen (2012–2019)

Axiom Zen was a venture studio, bootstrapped in 2012 by Roham Gharegozlou and Sam Gharegozlou in Vancouver. Within six months, the founding team was joined by several of its core members — including Dieter Shirley, co-author of the ERC-721 NFT standard and now CTO of Flow; and soon after by engineers who remain central to the organization today, including Alexander Hentschel (Chief Architect, Flow), Eric Lin (Head of Engineering, Dapper Labs), and Kan Zhang (Head of Engineering, Flow), among many others.

In the six years that Roham led Axiom Zen as CEO, the studio shipped dozens of products used by more than 200 million people worldwide, in partnership with major global brands. The defining characteristic was not volume — it was range. On every experimental platform that emerged during that period, Axiom Zen was among the first developers to build:

  • Augmented and mixed reality — early applications on Google Glass, Magic Leap, and Microsoft HoloLens, at a time when the developer ecosystems for these platforms barely existed. Early experiments around AR games led to CryptoKitties.
  • Crypto and blockchain — experimentation began in 2014, led by many members of what is now the Flow team. In 2014, the team entered and won the grand prize at the inaugural Money 20/20 hackathon in Las Vegas, submitting multiple projects including two Bitcoin-based prototypes.
  • AI and machine learning — working with Fortune 500 companies and startup teams on applied AI systems, years before the current wave of generative AI made the category mainstream.

This was not accidental breadth. The venture studio model demands it. When your job is to find the next wave before it breaks, you have to be in the water on every promising swell. Most won't work. The ones that do become companies.

The Spinouts

Several Axiom Zen ventures became standalone companies:

Routific — the studio's first spin-out. A route optimization platform for delivery logistics, it went through Techstars and later raised venture financing. It is still a successful business in Vancouver, led by Marc Kuo, one of the earliest members of the Axiom Zen team.

Timeline — one of the earliest attempts at using AI to generate bite-sized, impartially organized news delivered to mobile. The app was named Apple's Best New App in 88 countries and Editor's Choice in 10.

ZenHub — a project management solution deeply integrated into GitHub. It became the tool of choice for developer teams at scale, used by teams within Microsoft, Google, Salesforce, NASA, and IBM, among others. ZenHub was built because Axiom Zen needed it internally — external developers wanted it too, so it became a product.

And then there was CryptoKitties.

From Venture Studio to Product Company

CryptoKitties launched in November 2017 and became the world's first major consumer crypto application. It proved that non-fungible tokens were technically possible and culturally potent — and it broke Ethereum in the process. The smart contract was already deployed and couldn't be changed. Gas prices made the economics untenable. The Lighthouse had lit the way for an entire industry, but it couldn't become the sustainable business.

What followed was a sequence of pivots that tested every assumption in the process described in this document.

First pivot: the wallet. If Ethereum was too hostile for consumers, maybe an adapter layer could fix it. Dapper Wallet became the first smart contract wallet designed for consumers — the first to handle key recovery on behalf of users, the first to integrate credit card payments, the first to pay gas fees on behalf of consumers (later converted to a dollar subscription). It was a non-custodial wallet with custodial-quality UX — a genuine technical achievement. But operating on Ethereum remained prohibitively expensive, and demand for consumer use cases dried up when the cost of using them pushed out everyone except pure speculators.

Second pivot: the game. Before NBA Top Shot, the team built Cheese Wizards — a blockchain game with over 615 ETH in its prize pool at peak and a few thousand active players. Its Tournament smart contract was 24,536 bytes — just 40 bytes under the EVM's 24,576-byte limit — one of the most complex Ethereum contracts ever written. The point is not that technical complexity is good — it's that in emerging markets, you cannot always build on existing infrastructure. Sometimes you have to reinvent the wheel because the existing wheel doesn't turn.

Third pivot: the platform and the killer app — as one bet. The fundamental lesson of CryptoKitties and every product built on Ethereum was that the infrastructure couldn't support consumer-scale applications. In 2019, Dapper signed a deal with the NBA, and the two bets were made as one: build a new blockchain (Flow, with a novel multi-node architecture and a new resource-oriented programming language, Cadence) and use the NBA as its killer app. The platform and the product were the same decision — Flow needed a killer app to attract developers and users, and NBA Top Shot needed infrastructure that could actually handle consumer scale. Neither made sense without the other.

NBA Top Shot launched in beta in October 2020 and processed over $1 billion in transaction volume within its first two years. It proved the thesis that mainstream users could be brought on-chain through a product they actually wanted to use — credit card payments, no crypto jargon, no wallet setup. Millions of users created Flow wallets without ever knowing they were on a blockchain.

The team had spent over a decade building at the user level, but because the technology was always emerging, they had always been forced to build at the platform level too — whether middleware (ZenHub), infrastructure (Flow), or entirely new interaction paradigms (CryptoKitties).

That is the track record behind this process. Not a framework invented in a classroom — a framework extracted from a decade of building, breaking, pivoting, and rebuilding across every major emerging technology platform of the 2010s and 2020s.

How to Read This Document

This is a codification document — a comprehensive capture of the innovation process that produced CryptoKitties, NBA Top Shot, Flow blockchain, and Dapper Labs. It has never existed in one place before. It lived in pitch decks, scattered notes, verbal knowledge, and the accumulated intuition of people who had been through the process enough times to feel when something was right.

That era is over. The venture studio model only works when every person operates as a founder — not just in mindset, but in daily actions and decision-making. You cannot operate as a founder if the decision-making framework lives in someone else's head.

This version prioritizes fidelity over brevity. A separate, forward-looking operational playbook will be distilled later. This version captures everything the process has been up to now, including the parts that are still evolving.

Part I

The Thesis

Why Most Innovation Processes Fail — and What We Do Differently

Most people think the path to startup success is a straight line. It isn't. It's a tangled, recursive, often grueling process of building, failing, learning, and building again. The image that opens our playbook — the messy scribble versus the clean arrow — isn't decoration. It's the most honest slide in the deck.

The "secret" to startup success is that there is no secret. There is only a disciplined process for navigating chaos, applied with relentless consistency. Our process has nine steps. They are not magical. They are not even original — most draw on the work of thinkers we admire and will name throughout this document. What is original is how we sequence them, how we weigh them, and how we've pressure-tested them against real ventures that either succeeded or didn't.

The Nine Steps

  1. Constraints & Milestones — How much fuel do we have, and where do we have to get to in order to get more?
  2. North Star — What are we optimizing for? What are we optimizing against?
  3. Wave Spotting — What are the biggest technology waves right now, and where do they intersect with our North Star?
  4. Paddle Play — Get in the water. Build things. Play aggressively.
  5. Brainstorming — Don't say "no" (at first). Write everything down. Be naive. Iterate on customer × problem fits.
  6. The Culling — Kill as many ideas as possible with clear, documented reasoning.
  7. RATs — Riskiest Assumption Tests. Rubber meets the road. Define the biggest risks and start to de-risk.
  8. Clock Speed — Ship fast to learn fast. The speed of your learning loops is your primary competitive advantage.
  9. Strategy — The path to defensibility. A sustainable moat. But remember: strategy is the third mission, not the first.

These nine steps are sequential for any given idea — an idea starts at the top and works its way down. But they are not a waterfall, and they are not circular for a single idea. The critical distinction is between steps 1-6 and steps 7-9:

Steps 1-6 are thought experiments. This is where an idea goes from "just playing around" to a specific initiative with a defined shape. You're thinking, evaluating, and killing. No code. No users. No money spent beyond thinking time.

Step 7 onwards, you're in the market. Once you enter RATs, you're shipping, you're testing with real users, clock speed matters, and you keep iterating until the venture either succeeds or fails. Even at scale, a successful company needs to keep challenging the assumptions on which it's built — always looking for the riskiest things that could threaten it.

An individual idea doesn't go back to the beginning. But a company is constantly generating new ideas that start at Step 1. The process is designed to be cheap in the early stages and expensive only when you've earned the right to spend.

The Three Missions

Before we go deeper, one principle must be established. It governs the entire sequence:

First mission: Get the ship moving. Nothing else matters if the product doesn't exist. Build. Ship. Get it into the world. A mediocre product in the market teaches you more than a perfect product in your head.

Second mission: Get a "high clock speed" culture. Culture eats strategy for breakfast. The second priority — before strategy, before optimization, before scaling — is to build a team that learns faster than the competition. Clock speed is the only unfair advantage a startup has.

Third mission: Strategy. The rudder only works if the ship is moving. Strategy mainly matters post-PMF. Before product-market fit, your job is to find the wave and ride it. After product-market fit, your job is to build a moat around the position you've earned. Doing this out of order — strategizing before shipping, or optimizing before you have product-market fit — is the most common way smart people fail at startups.

The Portfolio Investment Framework

Not every project gets the same resources, the same constraints, or the same leash. We manage our portfolio in three tiers, and understanding which tier a project belongs to determines everything about how it's run.

Established Business Units (EBUs) are product lines with significant operational scale, revenue impact, and external commitments. They operate with full studio teams, multi-quarter roadmaps, and robust infrastructure. They require structured reporting. Examples: Dapper Social Studio (AKA DSS: Disney Pinnacle, NFL All Day, NBA Top Shot), Dapper Future of Finance (AKA DFF: Peak Money, FCM via FF).

Big Bet Projects (BBPs) are high-conviction product initiatives that have passed early-stage validation and now merit investment to prove TAM. Each represents a transformational opportunity with bounded risk, falsifiable assumptions, and structured review checkpoints. Examples: Miquela, CryptoKitties.

Sandbox Experiments and Research (SERs) are early-stage explorations, prototypes, or partner experiments designed to test foundational assumptions at very low cost — to "find the fun" and validate potential for high retention and virality. They are strictly non-committal and explicitly time-boxed. Examples: Aura, PDM, MCAP/Sayso (via AZ).

The governing principle: "Constraints breed greatness." Smaller projects have greater constraints (to move faster) and more frequent check-ins (to course correct faster). Larger projects can have more generous constraints (commensurate with their ROI) and less frequent check-ins (if course corrections are needed less frequently).

The mechanism for moving between tiers is the Green Light Framework — a series of validation milestones that unlock investment in tranches. We'll cover it in detail in Part V.

Part II

Finding the Wave

Step 1: Constraints & Milestones

"How much fuel do we have — and where do we have to get to in order to get more?"

Every project starts with a reality check. Before you brainstorm, before you spot waves, before you do anything creative, you must answer two questions: What resources do we have? And what milestone must we hit to earn more?

This is not bureaucracy. It is the single most important discipline in a venture studio, because the venture studio model runs on capital efficiency. We are not a single-product company that can pour everything into one bet. We run a portfolio. Every dollar and every engineer-hour allocated to one project is a dollar and an engineer-hour not allocated to another.

The Portfolio Investment Framework creates the guardrails. An SER gets a small team in skunkworks mode and a tight time box. A BBP gets a dedicated team working closely with leadership and prioritized resource access. An EBU gets a full studio. The key insight is that the severity of the constraint should match the maturity of the evidence. You earn more resources by proving your assumptions, not by arguing persuasively in a conference room.

Larger projects can have more generous constraints (commensurate with their predictability of ROI) and less frequent check-ins (depending on how often course corrections are needed and the team's ability to execute independently in response to new information).

When building costs drop by an order of magnitude, the same budget buys dramatically more experimentation. The constraint that matters most is no longer capital — it is the team's capacity to evaluate what those experiments produce.

Step 2: North Star

Begin with the end in mind.

Before you spot waves, you need to know which direction you're looking. The North Star answers two questions:

  • What are you optimizing for?
  • What are you optimizing against?

And then: What are the biggest waves / market shifts right now that intersect with that North Star?

The North Star is not strategy. It is not tactics. It is purpose — the reason the company exists and the future it is trying to create. When anyone can build anything quickly, the gravitational pull of a clear North Star is the only thing that prevents the team from chasing every prototype that comes out of a fast build cycle.

Dapper Labs North Star: Digital Freedom. We were conceived in 2017 and founded in 2018 on the thesis that crypto enables much more than just currency. We saw a world where crypto technology is as integral to daily life and the average business as the internet is. Crypto networks enable a better digital future for humanity — where individuals own their own data, digital assets, and online identities irrevocably; where creators and IP owners own the relationships with their communities; where the most important parts of the software we rely on for our everyday lives is in our own control, running on networks that anyone can access and build on, that everyone can trust and verify, and that no one can gate-keep, censor, or block.

Peak Money North Star: Democratize Opportunity. This generation watched the traditional path to wealth become mathematically out of reach. So we weaponized speculation. We turned investing into culture. The financial system is being rebuilt in our image: tokenized, programmable, social. We don't want another payments app. We want money working autonomously while we live our lives. Access to complex strategies without a Bloomberg terminal, access to the best yields without a broker — all as discoverable as a feed and as accessible as a swipe. No gatekeepers. This means building an anti-bank, not another neo-bank.

Collectibles North Star: Empower Fans.

Step 3: Wave Spotting

What waves are we surfing, and what is the stage of the wave in terms of the technology adoption S-curve?

Think of new technologies — AI, blockchain, VR, whatever's next — as big waves forming offshore. Most people don't see them yet, or don't know what to do with them. Our job is to be among the first to see them, understand them, and ride them successfully.

Any technology that is simultaneously accelerating your process and reshaping your market must be evaluated on both axes — as a tool and as a wave. Missing either lens means you are either building the future with yesterday's methods or building yesterday's product with tomorrow's tools.

Wave spotting is not trend-spotting. It is a rigorous analysis involving three lenses:

Lens 1: Platform Maturity vs. Application Readiness

"Gentlemen, there's only two ways I know of to make money: bundling and unbundling." — Jim Barksdale, Netscape (1995, during Netscape's IPO roadshow at the Savoy Hotel in London, when an investor asked how he could be sure Microsoft wouldn't bundle a browser into Windows. The investor was right — Microsoft did exactly that and destroyed Netscape. The quote outlived the company.)

Platform maturity determines the easiest plays. When a platform is immature, the opportunity is in building the foundation — the infrastructure itself. As it matures, the opportunity shifts to middleware, then to applications, then to aggregation. The cycle repeats.

The visual model shows S-curves stacking: each new infrastructure layer enables a spike of application innovation, which saturates, which creates demand for the next infrastructure layer. Understanding where you are on this curve — and more importantly, where you are about to be — is the first act of wave spotting.

For crypto, this meant recognizing that Ethereum's infrastructure layer was mature enough for applications (CryptoKitties, 2017) but not mature enough for scale (which led to building Flow). For AI, it means asking: are we at the infrastructure layer (building models), the middleware layer (building tools), or the application layer (building products end users touch)?

Lens 2: Technology Adoption Cycle

Two models interact here, and confusing them is a common mistake.

The Gartner Hype Cycle maps expectations over time: Innovation Trigger → Peak of Inflated Expectations → Trough of Disillusionment → Slope of Enlightenment → Plateau of Productivity. This tells you about market sentiment and timing risk.

The Technology Adoption Lifecycle (from Everett Rogers) maps users over time: Innovators → Early Adopters → [THE CHASM] → Early Majority → Late Majority → Laggards. This tells you about your go-to-market strategy.

The critical insight is that these cycles repeat and compound. A technology doesn't go through one hype cycle — it goes through many, each building on the last. Blockchain had a hype cycle around Bitcoin (2013), another around ICOs (2017), another around NFTs/DeFi (2021), and another is forming now around tokenization especially stablecoins. Each cycle reaches higher and builds more durable infrastructure than the last.

Understanding which hype cycle you're in — and where on the adoption curve your target users sit — determines everything about how you build and how you go to market.

Lens 3: The "Why Now?" Intersection

Every wave requires an intersection of enabling factors. Answering "Why Now?!" is not optional — it is the single most important question in idea evaluation, because timing kills more startups than bad ideas do.

The five factors:

  • Precursor Technologies — What enabling tech had to exist first?
  • Customer Behavior — Are people already showing they'll want this?
  • Infrastructure Readiness — Can the underlying systems support the product?
  • Economics — Will unit economics and CAC vs LTV make sense?
  • Regulation — Does the regulatory environment permit, tolerate, or overlook this?

For NBA Top Shot, the "Why Now?" was: Ethereum proved digital scarcity works (precursor tech), basketball fans were already spending on digital content and collectibles were resurging (customer behavior), Flow was nearing readiness (infrastructure), NFT minting costs were dropping (economics), and regulatory clarity on collectibles vs. securities was sufficient (regulation).

Current Waves in Direction of Our North Star

As of this writing, the biggest waves intersecting our North Star are:

  • Gen AI — for devs (composability), creators, consumers
  • Tokenization and adoption of stablecoin — personal finance

And, more localized to consumer crypto:

  • Asia — strong second-order effects across portfolio + Flow
  • Fungible coins — incentives, engagement, monetization

Each of these waves is mapped to existing and future products. CryptoKitties and Miquela explored the Gen AI wave. CryptoKitties and Top Shot rode the Asia wave. Fungible coins represent a future opportunity for Top Shot and beyond. Stablecoins and RWAs point toward Dapper's personal finance ambitions.

Part III

Getting in the Water

Step 4: Paddle Play

Just ship it. Play and have fun — aggressively!

Once you've spotted a promising wave, you don't stare at it. You get in the water. This is the Paddle Play — low-cost, high-frequency experimentation designed to build ground-truth understanding of the technology and the design space.

When prototyping costs approach zero, Paddle Play should become dramatically more aggressive — more experiments, faster kills, higher volume. The risk is not wasted resources; it is drowning in undifferentiated prototypes that all look plausible.

Paddle play is not undirected tinkering. It is a deliberate process with a specific goal: to develop an almost visceral intuition for what is possible. You cannot get this from reading reports. You get it from building.

But here is the critical distinction that most innovation frameworks miss: not all builds are the same kind of build. We have a taxonomy for this — what we call "Different Strokes" — and confusing them is one of the most expensive mistakes a startup team can make.

The Six Archetypes: Different Strokes

Every application built on top of a platform falls into one of six archetypes. Each has a different objective, a different metric, a different mindset, and — crucially — a different trap. If you treat a Petri Dish like a Killer App, you will run out of money. If you treat a Lighthouse like a Probe, you will miss the signal.

The moment of commitment is the most dangerous moment in a startup's life. Most founders fail here because they commit to the product before they commit to the objective. You must define the Shape of the Bet before you build.

A critical clarification: the point is not that every idea must pass through all six archetypes, or that you take something through different phases. Different bets will have different shapes — and that's fine. What matters is being clear with yourself and your team about what you're trying to accomplish and what success looks like. If that definition changes partway through, that's possible — but changing the shape of a bet mid-flight is genuinely hard. Ideally, you define success up front. If you're changing the definition of success, you need to reason through every assumption from first principles to make sure you're not trying to fit a square peg in a round hole.

1. The Petri Dish — "Get Weird"

Before there is strategy, there must be play. A Petri Dish is a throwaway environment designed for unstructured chaos. When we don't yet understand the underlying capabilities of the platform — or when the platform itself is still wet cement — we build Petri Dishes.

The goal is not ROI. The goal is not retention. The goal is learning — understanding the contours of what you're building. Groping in the dark. We are looking for interesting data, unexpected insights, and the weird-but-genuine use case that no PRD could have predicted.

Metric: Learning velocity — new understanding, interesting data points, surprising user behaviors.

Mindset: If it breaks, good. If it's strange, better. If you learned something you didn't know yesterday, it worked.

Phase: Paddle Play.

2. The Probe — "Falsifiable Hypotheses"

A Probe is a structured experiment designed to purchase information. You don't always start with a Petri Dish — if you already understand the design space, you start here. The Petri Dish is only necessary when you're genuinely groping in the dark. But almost every idea must pass through a Probe phase before advancing to any of the later archetypes.

Here, we trade intuition for the scientific method. We have a hypothesis — e.g., "Users will pay for scarcity" — and we build the thinnest possible slice to validate or invalidate it. A Probe has high uncertainty but strict parameters. It is a tracer bullet fired into the dark to see if it hits metal or air.

Metric: Learning velocity. Did we validate the hypothesis?

Mindset: Dispassionate rigor. A failed Probe that generates clear data is a success.

Phase: Paddle Play.

3. The Frame — "Drawing the Edges"

A platform without boundaries is a void. Developers and users freeze when they can do "anything." They need to know what is possible and how to interact with the system.

A Frame application exists to define the physics of the platform. It demonstrates the constraints and the capabilities. It teaches the market the interaction language of the new technology.

Example: Microsoft Solitaire. People think Solitaire was included in Windows to let office workers slack off. It wasn't. It was a Frame. In 1990, the mouse was a terrifying new input device. Solitaire was built to frame the boundary of the user interface: it taught the world how to drag and drop. It framed the capability of the OS in a loop of simple delight. The design was deliberate: the core mechanic required precisely the interaction the OS needed users to learn.

Note on Wii Sports: Nintendo's Wii Sports (2006) is sometimes cited as a Frame — it taught users motion controls through simple sports. But it was more than a Frame: it was part of the core value proposition for consumers, not just a demonstration for developers. Wii Sports sold 82.9 million copies and made gamers out of millions who had never touched a controller. That makes it closer to a Killer App that happened to also serve a framing function. The distinction matters: a pure Frame exists to educate; Wii Sports existed to delight — and educated as a side effect.

Metric: User competence and developer understanding.

Mindset: Pedagogical. We are teaching the world how to use us.

Phase: Brainstorm phase. Only relevant if you own the platform.

Trap: Trying to monetize too early. Frames are often loss leaders for education.

4. The Beachhead — "Dominate the Niche"

You cannot boil the ocean. To win the war, you must first secure a toe-hold. A Beachhead is an application designed to dominate a small, specific, often non-obvious segment of the market so completely that it grants you the resources and legitimacy to attack adjacent territory.

This is the Innovator's Dilemma in action — disrupting from the edges of the market. You don't attack the incumbent head-on; you dominate a niche they don't care about. Crossing the Chasm comes later, after you've secured your beachhead and need to expand into the mainstream. The Beachhead itself is about insiders and early adopters — the people who are desperate enough for a solution that they'll tolerate an imperfect one.

Example: Amazon Books. Jeff Bezos didn't start the Everything Store. He started a bookstore. Books were the beachhead — durable, easy to ship, infinite SKU variety. By dominating books, he built the logistics infrastructure to eventually swallow retail whole.

Example: Uber Black Car. Travis Kalanick didn't start by trying to replace the global taxi infrastructure. His Beachhead was the Black Car service in San Francisco — targeting high-net-worth tech workers who hated calling dispatchers. It was a tiny, high-margin, high-frequency niche. By dominating that beachhead, he built the logistics software and brand trust required to launch UberX and conquer the world.

Example: Salesforce's SMBs. When Benioff launched Salesforce, he didn't attack Siebel Systems at the Enterprise level. He couldn't — the IT departments were too entrenched. His Beachhead was small-to-medium businesses and individual sales teams who didn't have an IT department. He sold them "No Software" — just a login. He secured the low-end wedge, then moved upmarket.

Example: Facebook at Harvard (2004). Zuckerberg launched exclusively for Harvard students. Within 24 hours, 1,200 registered. Within a month, over half of Harvard undergraduates had joined. Instead of competing with MySpace (millions of users), Facebook established a beachhead where MySpace was weak: elite university campuses where a verified .edu identity created trust and exclusivity. By the time it opened to the public in September 2006, it had 6 million users and a defensible brand identity.

Metric: Market share within the defined niche (not total TAM).

Mindset: Conquest. Secure the ground, then expand.

Phase: Brainstorm phase.

Trap: Staying on the beach too long. A Beachhead is a landing zone, not a home.

5. The Lighthouse — "Safe Harbor for Builders"

A platform dies without developers. But developers are risk-averse; they need to know it's safe to build. A Lighthouse application is built to clear the way — to prove technical feasibility, show where the rocks are, and signal to the ecosystem: "Look, we built this complex thing on our protocol, and it works. You can build here too."

Example: CryptoKitties. Before CryptoKitties, the ERC-721 standard was a theory. CryptoKitties didn't just sell cats; it was a Lighthouse that proved non-fungible tokens were technically possible and culturally potent. It lit the way for the entire NFT industry.

Example: Toy Story (1995). Pixar's Toy Story proved that a feature-length, fully computer-animated film could be technically executed, emotionally resonant, and commercially viable. Budget: $30M. Worldwide gross: $373M. It catalyzed the entire CGI animation industry — Disney transitioned from hand-drawn, DreamWorks launched Shrek, Pixar's RenderMan became an industry standard. Every animation studio on earth invested in CGI capabilities because of this one Lighthouse.

Metric: Developer influx and second-party adoption.

Mindset: Demonstration. Show, don't tell.

Phase: Brainstorm phase. Only relevant if you own the platform.

Trap: Complexity. A Lighthouse must be bright and visible, not obscure.

6. The Killer App — "Network Effects"

The holy grail. The Killer App is the product that makes the platform indispensable. It kick-starts the network effects and the flywheel. It creates the liquidity, the volume, and the social graph that locks users in.

A Killer App justifies the existence of the infrastructure. It creates a gravitational pull so strong that the friction of adoption becomes irrelevant.

Example: NBA Top Shot. Top Shot wasn't just a collectible; it was the proof of concept for digital ownership at scale. It brought the masses on-chain, creating the volume and excitement that powered the Flow network.

Example: VisiCalc for the Apple II (1979). The textbook killer app. Dan Bricklin watched a Harvard Business School professor erase and rewrite entries on a blackboard and built a spreadsheet for the Apple II. When told the $100 software required a $2,000 computer, customers added the Apple II to the order. Over 25% of Apple IIs sold in 1979 were reportedly purchased for VisiCalc. It crossed the Apple II from hobby computer to business tool and prompted IBM to build the PC.

Example: Halo for Xbox (2001). Microsoft acquired Bungie specifically to have a launch title for the Xbox. Halo shipped alongside the console, sold alongside half of every Xbox sold, and single-handedly legitimized Microsoft's entry into the console market. The term "Halo killer" entered gaming vocabulary. Without Halo, the Xbox platform likely would not have survived.

Metric: Exponential growth, retention, and K-factor (virality).

Mindset: Scale.

Phase: Brainstorm phase.

Trap: Premature scaling. If you try to build a Killer App before you have the infrastructure to support it, you will collapse under your own weight.

Which Archetype When?

The six archetypes map to the innovation process in a specific sequence:

  • Paddle Play uses Petri Dishes and Probes. These are cheap, fast, and disposable. Their purpose is learning, not launching.
  • Brainstorming targets Beachheads, Lighthouses, and Killer Apps. These are the products you're actually considering building.
  • Frame and Lighthouse are only relevant if you own the platform. If you're building on someone else's infrastructure, you don't need to teach the market how to use it — that's the platform owner's job.

The archetype decision tree:

  • Do we understand the design space? No → Petri Dish. Yes → continue.
  • Do we have a falsifiable hypothesis? Yes → Probe. No → back to Petri Dish.
  • Do we own the platform? Yes → Do developers need a signal? Yes → Lighthouse. Do users need to learn the interaction model? Yes → Frame.
  • Is there a defensible niche we can dominate? Yes → Beachhead.
  • Can we ignite network effects? Yes → Killer App.

The sequence is not always linear. CryptoKitties started as something between a Petri Dish and a Probe, became a Lighthouse, and pointed the way toward the Killer App (Top Shot). The archetypes describe the objective of what you're building, not a permanent classification.

Part IV

Generating and Killing Ideas

Step 5: Brainstorming

Don't say "no" (at first). Write everything down. Be naive.

With a foundational understanding built through hands-on Paddle Play, the mode shifts to expansive, bottoms-up ideation. The objective is to generate a high volume of potential configurations — ways to surf the wave.

When idea generation is cheap and fast, the framing of the problem becomes the binding constraint. That makes the quality of the Blue Hat phase — defining the problem precisely — the single highest-leverage activity in the entire brainstorm. Garbage framing in, garbage ideas out, just faster.

Three rules govern this phase:

  1. Suspend disbelief. No idea is too impractical at this stage. Quantity is a prerequisite for quality.
  2. Write everything down. Maintain a massive pipeline. This isn't just brainstorming — it's cultivating a team mindset that constantly sees opportunities.
  3. Expect a 90-95% kill rate. For every ten ideas, maybe nine won't go anywhere. That's not failure. That's efficient exploration of the solution space.

The critical discipline is customer-backward problem definition. Every idea must be articulated from the perspective of a specific customer and the problem it solves. We use a structured pitch format:

"For [This Specific Type of Person] who really needs to [Solve This Annoying Problem or Get This Important Thing Done], [Name of Product/Service] is a [website, app, service] that gives them [The main awesome thing it does for them]. Unlike [How it's done now / the main competitors], we're the only ones who [What makes us better and different in the eyes of the customer]."

This format forces three things: specificity about the user, clarity about the problem, and honesty about differentiation. If you can't fill in these blanks crisply, the idea isn't ready for evaluation — it's still a vague notion.

The Six Thinking Hats (Adapted)

We structure group brainstorms using an adapted version of Edward de Bono's Six Thinking Hats. The critical rule: everyone should be focused on the same hat at the same time. Mixing modes — having one person generate ideas while another critiques them — kills creativity and creates political dynamics that have nothing to do with idea quality.

The sequence:

  1. Blue Hat (Process) — Focus the most time here. Define the problem clearly. Agree on the process. Set the frame before generating content.
  2. White Hat (Facts) — Break and gather facts and data if you're missing them. Don't brainstorm in an information vacuum.
  3. Red Hat (Feelings) — Start the creative work by encouraging feelings and emotion. Time-box this. Gut reactions matter — they encode pattern-matching that hasn't been articulated yet.
  4. Green Hat (Ideas) — An open session where everyone suggests ideas. No judgment. Record everything. Clarify where needed but do not evaluate.
  5. Yellow Hat (Positivity) — A positive session where everyone helps improve the case for each other's ideas. Make everything communal. This is where a mediocre idea becomes a good idea through collective intelligence.
  6. Black Hat (Analysis) — Finally, we analyze. With a sober hat, document all the greatest risks we see to each idea with a skeptical eye. This comes last because premature criticism kills more good ideas than bad execution does.

Step 6: The Culling

Kill as many ideas as possible with clear and documented reasoning.

The divergent phase of brainstorming is followed by a sharp pivot to convergent thinking. The objective now is to kill ideas as efficiently as possible by rigorously examining their underlying assumptions and inherent risks.

When any competent team with access to the same tools can build a version of your idea in weeks, the kill filters matter more than ever. If the idea has no path to a power that cannot be replicated by tools alone, it is already dead — you just haven't received the news yet.

This is not about being negative. It is about being honest. Every hour you spend on an idea that should have been killed is an hour stolen from an idea that could have worked. The frameworks below are applied in parallel — each is a different lens, and an idea must survive all of them.

Kill Filter 1: Vitamin, Painkiller, or Candy?

Is this a nice-to-have (vitamin), a solution to a burning pain (painkiller), or something people crave because it's fun (candy)?

Vitamins are the hardest products to build businesses around. People know they should take them, but they forget, they deprioritize, they churn. Painkillers sell themselves — when your head is splitting, you don't comparison-shop. But there's a third category that matters enormously for entertainment and consumer products: Candy. Everybody loves candy. Nobody needs candy, but people will pay for it eagerly, repeatedly, and with genuine delight.

This distinction matters because most entertainment and gaming products will never be painkillers — and that's fine. CryptoKitties wasn't solving a burning pain. NBA Top Shot wasn't curing a headache. They were candy — irresistible, novel, fun. The standard Vitamin/Painkiller framework would kill every entertainment product at the Culling stage. Adding Candy to the taxonomy lets entertainment products pass the filter on their own terms: not because they solve a problem, but because the craving is real and the engagement is intense.

Using the Jobs-to-be-Done lens: what job is the user hiring this product to do? Is it a job they desperately need done (painkiller), a job they know they should do (vitamin), or a job they want to do because it's fun, exciting, or status-conferring (candy)?

Kill Filter 2: CAC vs. LTV

What are the projected unit economics? Can you acquire customers at a cost that is sustainably lower than their lifetime value?

This requires early hypotheses about acquisition channels and monetization — even rough ones. A good business model has LTV substantially greater than CAC. A poor business model has the reverse. If you can't articulate even a rough theory of how LTV will exceed CAC, the idea is not ready for investment.

Kill Filter 3: Innovator's Dilemma

Does this idea leverage an asymmetry that allows you to attack incumbents where they are weakest or unwilling to move? Clayton Christensen's insight — which he later reframed as the Innovator's Opportunity — is that disruptive innovations start by serving overlooked or underserved segments with simpler, cheaper, or more convenient solutions. Incumbents rationally ignore them because the margins are too low and the market too small. By the time the disruption scales up the performance S-curve and begins competing for mainstream customers, it's too late.

The kill question: Are incumbents structurally unable or unwilling to do what we're doing? If the answer is no — if a well-resourced competitor could just copy us — this is not a Christensen play.

Kill Filter 4: Product-Team Fit

This is the most intuitive and least quantifiable filter, which makes it the easiest to skip and the most expensive to ignore. Product-Team Fit asks: is this specific team the right team to build this specific product?

The Chicago Bulls won six championships not because they had the best individual players in the league, but because they had the right combination for the system they ran. Jordan's scoring, Pippen's versatility, Rodman's rebounding — each was elite at something the system demanded.

Product-Team Fit includes:

  • Domain expertise — Does the team have deep knowledge of the problem space, or will they be learning from zero?
  • Technical capability — Can the team actually build the hard parts, or will they be blocked by technical challenges?
  • Founder-market passion — Is the team genuinely obsessed with this problem, or is it an intellectual exercise?
  • Speed and culture match — Does the team's working style match what the product demands? A high-clock-speed consumer product needs a different team than a slow, methodical enterprise product.

Kill Filter 5: The 7 Powers (Hamilton Helmer)

We use Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers as a kill filter — not to determine whether an idea is good, but to determine whether it can build a sustainable business. An idea without a plausible path to at least one Power is an idea that will eventually be competed away to zero profit.

The seven powers:

  1. Scale Economies — Unit costs decline as volume, density, or scope increases.
  2. Network Economies — Customers benefit when new customers sign up (direct) or complements become available (indirect). Can be insurmountably huge (Facebook) or very localized. Often leads to winner-take-all dynamics.
  3. Counter-Positioning — Incumbents will not adopt your approach because doing so would damage their current model. This is NOT the same as disruption (more on this in the Strategy chapter). A stand-alone unattractive proposition is not counter-positioning. The key test: would a rational incumbent choose not to copy you?
  4. Switching Costs — The product is sufficiently embedded in customers' lives that changing is costly. Three types: Financial, Procedural, Relational. Increase switching costs by introducing complementary products and services.
  5. Branding — Creates either a "feel good" association or a "reduction in uncertainty." Enables pricing power.
  6. Cornered Resource — Advantaged access to a resource critical to the business. This includes intellectual property and a critical team who have unquestioned loyalty to the company.
  7. Process Power — The development over time (with many, many decisions throughout the organization) of company processes that both confer significant benefits and, due to their intrinsic complexity, cannot be easily emulated. Not just operational excellence — it must be genuinely hard to copy.

The Power Progression: Different powers become available at different stages of a company's life:

  • Origination (early stage): Cornered Resources, Counter-Positioning
  • Take-Off (growth stage): Scale Economies, Network Economies, Switching Costs
  • Stability (mature stage): Branding, Process Power

At the Culling stage, you're asking: does this idea have a plausible path to one or more of these powers? You don't need the power yet — you need to see how you could get there.

The critical equation: P = MC for businesses with no power. Over time, in competitive markets, profits erode to zero for companies that have no structural advantage. In high-growth environments, competitive arbitrage is delayed (e.g., the early PC boom), which is why startups can survive without strategy temporarily. But you cannot thrive without it permanently.

Hamilton Helmer — who was on the board of Dapper Labs in the early days, and was my Econ 101 professor at Stanford — always used to tell me: "The rudder doesn't work if the ship is not moving." That's such a good statement because it shows that even a lifelong academic and strategist believed the most important thing is to get the ship moving. Once it's moving, you can decide where to point it.

The output of the Culling is not just a go/no-go. It is a clear articulation of the most significant assumptions that need validation — the inputs to the next step.

Part V

De-Risking and Validation

Step 7: RATs (Riskiest Assumption Tests)

Rubber meets the road. We start to define the biggest risks and assumptions and start to de-risk.

Ideas that survive the Culling enter the phase where we stop theorizing and start testing. RATs — Riskiest Assumption Tests — are the bridge between "this idea seems promising" and "we have evidence it works."

Compressed build cycles mean you can run more RATs in parallel and in sequence. The discipline shifts from "can we afford to test this?" to "are we testing the right assumption in the right order?"

The principle is simple: identify the assumption that, if wrong, would kill the idea. Test that assumption first. If it holds, move to the next riskiest assumption. If it fails, either pivot or kill.

We use five frameworks to structure the de-risking process:

Framework 1: Hooks, Legs, Flywheel

This is our own internal framework for understanding what makes a product viable. (It shares DNA with related models like Nir Eyal's Hook Model and Brian Balfour's growth loops, but was developed independently. Understanding those frameworks provides useful context for why this approach works.) It maps directly to the Green Light Framework stages and provides the operational lens for how we think about product-market fit.

Hook — How does the product capture initial attention and draw users in? What is the compelling entry point? At the testing stage: creative tests, click-through tests, value proposition experiments. The question is simple: Does anyone care?

Legs — What mechanics or value proposition will build habits and retain users over time? Testing progresses sequentially: First 5 minutes → D1 retention → D2 → W1 → W4. You optimize each stage before moving to the next. The question: Is it fun? Enough to form habits?

Flywheel — How is growth built into the product itself, creating a self-reinforcing loop? Testing moves through: Conversion → Activation → Hype/virality → Public launch. The question: Can it grow?

The power of this framework is its sequential focus. You don't optimize the flywheel before you have legs. You don't optimize legs before you have a hook. Each stage must be proven before you invest in the next.

Framework 2: Lean Startup (Eric Ries)

The Build-Measure-Learn cycle is the heartbeat of the RATs phase. We build the smallest thing that tests our riskiest assumption, measure the result, and learn whether to persevere or pivot.

The critical nuance: an MVP (Minimum Viable Product) is not a crappy version of the full product. It is the minimum build required to test a specific hypothesis. If you're testing whether users will pay for digital scarcity, you don't need a fully designed marketplace — you need a way for someone to pay for something scarce. Everything else is waste at this stage.

Framework 3: AAARRR (Pirate Metrics)

Dave McClure's Pirate Metrics funnel — Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Revenue, Referral — provides a complementary lens to Hooks, Legs, Flywheel:

  • Acquisition → relates to Hook (getting people in the door)
  • Activation → relates to Hook (the "aha moment" in the first session)
  • Retention → relates to Legs (do they come back?)
  • Revenue → relates to Flywheel (can we monetize?)
  • Referral → relates to Flywheel (do they bring others?)

The practical value of AAARRR is that it gives you specific, measurable stages to optimize one at a time.

Framework 4: Crossing the Chasm (Geoffrey Moore)

For products requiring a significant behavioral shift — which describes most of what we build — Moore's framework is essential. The key insight: there is a chasm between early adopters (who buy into the vision) and the early majority (who want a pragmatic, complete solution).

The Bowling Alley strategy applies directly to the Beachhead archetype: identify a single, specific "headpin" customer segment that desperately needs your product. Solve their problem completely — not mostly, completely — with a "whole product" solution. Dominate that niche. Then use that dominance as a beachhead to expand to adjacent segments.

At Dapper Labs, we are in the business of creating things that haven't existed before. We build products in markets where playbooks don't exist, pushing the boundaries of consumer behavior as much as technology. This means the Chasm is not theoretical for us — it is the central challenge of every product we launch.

The Echo Chamber of Hype is the Chasm's Abyss. In crypto and emerging technology, there is a specific failure mode that Moore's original framework didn't anticipate: hype-driven adoption that mimics early majority adoption but is actually just an amplified early adopter wave. The echo chamber of social media and speculation can make a product look like it has crossed the chasm when it hasn't. The crash that follows is not a market correction — it is the chasm reasserting itself. The lesson: measure real retention and utility, not hype-driven volume.

Framework 5: Innovator's Dilemma (Applied)

If your idea leverages a disruptive approach, RATs must specifically test whether you're actually serving overlooked niches or creating new markets — not just building a worse version of an incumbent product. The Innovator's Dilemma applied is about finding the specific customers who are over-served by existing solutions (and thus willing to trade features for cost/convenience) or non-consumers who can't access existing solutions at all.

The Green Light Framework (GLF)

The GLF is the formal mechanism for unlocking investment. It is simply a series of validation milestones that unlock investment ("reduce constraints") in tranches. Early on, GLF is centered around user-centric KPIs: typically starting with Hook, Legs, Flywheel. Later, investment is driven mainly by profitability.

Milestones must be material — and undeniable.

The stages for a consumer product:

Administering the Green Light Framework

The GLF is not just a stage-gate diagram — it is a financing mechanism with specific operational mechanics. Understanding how to administer it is as important as understanding the stages themselves.

Treat every request for resources as a financing. When a team asks for budget, headcount, or time, they are pitching investors. Every presentation should be treated like a board presentation or a pitch to lead investors. The standard is high because the capital is scarce and the opportunity cost is real.

Green-lighting a phase creates a check-in calendar. At the moment we approve a specific sprint or experiment, we simultaneously schedule the next demo day. "Here's $100K and two weeks to run this experiment. In two weeks, you will present progress. If the results merit continued funding, you continue. If not, you wind the project down and the team moves to something else." The cadence is tight — 10 days, two weeks, three weeks — whatever the experiment demands.

If funding is not renewed, the project winds down gracefully. Team members become free agents. They can jump into a new project or work on an evergreen company initiative. In the Axiom Zen days, the consulting practice served this function — sharpening tools and building skills between product development sprints. Today, the equivalent is a maintained backlog of high-value work that can absorb talent between bets.

Promotion between tiers follows the same logic. An SER earns BBP status by demonstrating enough GLF progress to justify a larger investment. A BBP earns EBU status when it has proven revenue and operational scale. The mechanism is always the same: evidence unlocks resources. Not arguments. Not politics. Not enthusiasm. Evidence.

Suggested Quantitative Benchmarks for Consumer Products

Hook (Stage 1): Remember — the product may not even exist yet. You can't survey users about a product they haven't used. What you can do:

  1. Run ads for the product and pretend it exists. Measure click-through rates. If people click, they care. >20% CTR on value prop tests is strong signal.
  2. Put the concept in front of people and observe their behavior. Don't ask for opinions — people's stated opinions are historically unreliable. Instead, watch: Do they lean in with curiosity or look confused? Do they keep talking about it after they don't have to? Do they call back with good questions, or just disappear?

The behavioral signals matter more than survey data. Curiosity, unprompted follow-up, and enthusiasm that persists after the meeting ends — those are Hook confirmation.

Legs (Stage 2): At this stage, we're not measuring D30 or D60. We're measuring something much simpler: will they come back for a second session later that day or the next day? A third session within a day or two after that?

Consumer products need high-frequency usage. If the product has legs like a centipede — people want to use it all day, and the product enables that behavior — that's perfect. If it's hard to imagine people coming back day after day, the job is going to be much harder.

Part VI

Shipping and Learning

Step 8: Clock Speed

Ship fast to learn fast.

Once an idea demonstrates a viable path and initial positive signals, the emphasis shifts to maximizing "clock speed" — the rate at which the team learns and iterates. This is not about moving fast and breaking things. It is about maximizing the number of learning loops per unit time. When the floor for iteration speed rises for everyone, clock speed alone is no longer a differentiator. The advantage migrates from cycle speed to cycle quality — the precision of what you measure and the rigor of how you act on it.

The visual model is a cycle — Measure → Feedback → Modify → Do/Redo — repeating as fast as possible. "Ship Fast to Learn Fast" means running this loop four times while your competitor runs it once. That's the advantage. That's the moat while you're building the real moat.

Have estimates for every metric expected before you go live. This is the Total Metrics Funnel discipline. You don't ship blind — you ship with a hypothesis about what each metric should look like. Then you measure the delta between expectation and reality. The delta is the learning.

Shipping in Slices

The unit of shipping is the slice — the smallest deliverable that generates a learning. Slices come in two categories:

Engineering Slices (in order of commitment):

  1. Prototype — Proof of concept. Does the core mechanic work at all?
  2. Tracer Bullet — De-risks tech feasibility. Can we build the critical path end to end?
  3. Closed Alpha — Real users, controlled environment. Do they behave as expected?
  4. MVP — Minimum viable product. The smallest thing that tests the full value proposition.
  5. Bold Beat — A significant, visible feature or update designed to elicit strong market feedback and test core hypotheses at scale.

Non-Engineering Slices (often faster and cheaper):

  1. Expert Interviews — Domain specialists who've seen this problem before.
  2. User + Market Research — Qualitative understanding of user needs and behaviors.
  3. Value Prop Testing — Ads, landing pages, and other methods to test whether the value proposition resonates before building anything.
  4. Paper Prototyping — Low-fidelity mockups tested with real users.

The non-engineering slices are underrated. Before you write a line of code, you can often answer critical questions by talking to the right people, showing them a sketch, or running an ad campaign for a product that doesn't exist yet. The fastest learning loop is the one that doesn't require a deploy.

Bold Beats: Turning One-Way Doors Into Two-Way Doors

There is a tension in the clock speed philosophy that must be confronted directly: shipping fast can become a trap if all you ship is incremental. The relentless pursuit of speed can lead teams down a path of incrementalism — small features, minor tweaks, safe bets. Shipping frequently is necessary but not sufficient. The critical question is: what are we shipping?

Bold Failure > Timid Success.

A Bold Beat — a concept originally coined by Mark Pincus (Zynga) and Bing Gordon (EA), and adapted for our needs at Dapper — is a time-limited product experience that uses "in-universe" framing instead of signaling experimentation to the customer. It has a falsifiable hypothesis. And it prevents feature bloat because the experience has a built-in expiration date.

This is the key insight most teams miss: a Bold Beat is not just a big ship. It is a ship with a defined end date and a clear narrative, so you can experiment at scale without permanently bloating the product. In effect, bold beats turn one-way doors into two-way doors.

"You can't win unless you learn how to lose." — Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, 6× NBA Champion

Why Bold Beats Matter

In a typical feature rollout, once something goes live, you're stuck with it. Pulling it back confuses users and creates friction. Bold Beats invert that dynamic:

  1. Time-Limited by Design. The campaign has a stated end date — tied to a season, a cultural moment, a tournament. Users expect it will end.
  2. In-Universe Framing. We never tell users "this is a test" or "this is in beta." Nobody likes feeling like a guinea pig. Instead, we frame the experience as a fully developed, thematic event — a "Summer Spectacular," a "Playoff Prediction Challenge," a "Legendary Drop." The user sees an event. We see an experiment.
  3. Falsifiable Hypothesis. Every Bold Beat tests a specific hypothesis at scale. "Will users pay for predictions tied to real outcomes?" "Does a daily challenge mechanic improve D7 retention?" The hypothesis is stated before launch and measured rigorously.
  4. Clean Exit. If the Beat succeeds, we bring it back — bigger, better, possibly permanent. If it fails, it sunsets at the advertised end date. No awkward deprecation. No user backlash. No feature bloat.

The Bold Beat Lifecycle

  1. Concept & Theme: Identify an upcoming event or narrative hook that fits a natural timeframe — a playoff series, a holiday, a community milestone.
  2. Rapid Development: Build the core experience quickly, focusing on the critical user journey. Integrate analytics to capture every relevant metric. Don't over-engineer — this is not designed for indefinite support.
  3. Launch & Engagement: Go live with a clear start and end date. Frame it as a special event with relevant visuals, exclusive rewards, and a compelling narrative.
  4. Real-Time Monitoring: Watch engagement closely. Adjust on the fly if you see friction. Gather qualitative feedback from the most engaged users.
  5. Conclusion: At the stated end date, the experience gracefully rolls off. Thank participants. Highlight achievements. Tease future possibilities.
  6. Post-Mortem & Decision: Compile data. Did the hypothesis hold? Based on the evidence, decide: refine and relaunch, or archive as a valuable learning. Either way, the main product remains clean.

Bold Beats and Culture

Beyond the product benefits, Bold Beats reshape internal culture:

  • They reduce the fear of failure. Because the experiment is time-limited and narratively framed, there's minimal stigma if it doesn't work.
  • They embrace creative risk. Teams become braver in design, marketing, and UX choices when they know the feature has a built-in exit.
  • They enable decentralized ownership. Different squads can each run their own short-term event with clear scope and clean boundaries — no cross-team maintenance dependencies piling up.
  • They align with "Do Things That Don't Scale." For a short campaign, you can hack together custom solutions, even manually orchestrate certain aspects, because you're not committing to indefinite support. If the concept resonates, invest in robust engineering later.

Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Unclear timeframes. If the end date is ambiguous, users feel duped when the feature disappears. Weave the timeline into the narrative.
  • Excessive complexity. The short window should encourage simpler, more experimental designs. Don't over-scope.
  • Failing to collect data. A Bold Beat is mainly a research opportunity. Map the entire user journey. Set up analytics before launch, not after.
  • No follow-through plan. Before launching, pre-write the next-steps document: what happens if it succeeds, what happens if it fails, who makes the call.

The insight is counterintuitive: celebrating big swings and learning how to lose is the path to rapid learning and startup success. A Bold Beat that fails spectacularly and clearly teaches more than a timid tweak that succeeds ambiguously. The enemy of learning is not failure — it is mediocrity. Ship things that make the metrics move violently in one direction or another. That's signal. A 2% improvement is noise.

Founders, Not Functions

Speed is every startup's only unfair advantage. To maintain speed, every role — from marketing to community to product management — needs to engage founder mode.

"There are two ways to run a company: founder mode and manager mode. New products fail when manager mode smothers founder mode." — Paraphrased from Paul Graham, "Founder Mode" (September 2024)

The Founders Not Functions philosophy means that at the early stage, titles don't matter. Everyone is obsessed with learning and solving the problem, not just doing their defined job. The person who ships the feature also reads the user feedback. The marketer also does user research interviews. The engineer also thinks about monetization. Everyone owns the problem end to end.

This doesn't scale forever. As products mature and move from SER to BBP to EBU, you need specialization. But the founder mindset must persist even as roles differentiate — the orientation toward learning, speed, and ownership doesn't go away. It just gets channeled into more structured forms.

Part VII

Building the Moat

Step 9: Strategy

"Strategy explains the ability of market players to create consistent, sustainable business value."

Strategy is the path to defensibility — a sustainable moat. But it must be understood in its proper place:

Strategy is NOT why we're here or what our goal is. That's our purpose / vision / mission — the North Star. Dapper's North Star is "Digital Freedom." That is not a strategy. It is a reason to exist.

Strategy is NOT what we're doing or how we're doing it. Those are tactics / methods / processes — the first eight steps of our innovation process. Building an MVP, running a Bold Beat, optimizing retention — those are tactics, not strategy.

Strategy explains how you will create business value that competitors cannot erode. It is the answer to: once you've found product-market fit and built something people want, how do you keep competitors from copying you and driving your profits to zero?

The brutal truth: P = MC for businesses with no power. Over time, profits erode to zero and many businesses will not return their cost of capital. In high-growth environments, competitive arbitrage is delayed — you can survive without strategy when the tide is rising fast enough. But you cannot thrive without it.

The 7 Powers Framework (Hamilton Helmer)

We use Helmer's 7 Powers not just as a culling filter (Step 6) but as the definitive framework for studying and building strategy. Each power represents a different form of sustainable competitive advantage, and each becomes available at different stages of a company's lifecycle.

At Origination (early stage), two powers are available:

Cornered Resource — Advantaged access to resources critical to the business. This includes intellectual property and a critical team who have unquestioned loyalty to the company. The Pixar model: Ed Catmull's technical genius, Steve Jobs' business acumen, and John Lasseter's creative vision constituted a cornered resource that no competitor could assemble. For Dapper: our relationships with the NBA, NFL, Disney/Pinnacle, and other IP holders; our engineering team's deep expertise in blockchain consumer products; our early-mover knowledge of what works in digital collectibles.

Counter-Positioning — A newcomer adopts a superior business model which the incumbent does not mimic due to anticipated damage to their existing business. The key distinction: counter-positioning is not the same as disruption.

Counter-Positioning vs. Disruption:

  • Five Guys vs. McDonalds — Counter-positioning. McDonalds could serve fresh, high-quality burgers but doing so would destroy their supply chain economics and speed model.
  • Netflix vs. HBO — Counter-positioning. HBO's parent (Time Warner) couldn't embrace streaming without cannibalizing cable subscriptions.
  • Kodak vs. Digital — Counter-positioning NOT counter-positioning. Kodak failed not because the digital business model would damage their film business (it would), but because digital photography as a standalone business was unattractive to Kodak at the time. The problem was cognitive bias and organizational inertia, not a rational economic calculation against self-cannibalization.

The counter-positioning decision tree:

  1. Is the new approach stand-alone attractive? No → Don't Invest. This is not counter-positioning.
  2. Yes → Would it be negative NPV for the incumbent to enter? Yes (Negative) → Don't Invest. This is counter-positioning — type "Milk." The incumbent will rationally let you take the market rather than cannibalize themselves.
  3. Joint NPV is Positive → Is there a further decrement? None → Invest (no CP advantage). Agency (job security) → Don't invest — CP exists because management is protecting their jobs. Cognitive Bias → Don't invest — CP exists because incumbent is "History's Slave," trapped by past mental models.

When counter-positioning is high, scale rapidly to reach other Powers before incumbents overcome their reluctance.

At Take-Off (growth stage), three additional powers become available:

Network Economies — Customers benefit when new customers sign up (direct network effects) or when complements become available (indirect). This is the most powerful form of competitive advantage in technology. Facebook's network effects made it nearly impossible to displace once critical mass was reached. But network effects can also be very localized — a ride-sharing app's network effects only matter within a given city.

Scale Economies — Unit costs decline as volume, density, or scope increases. Classic and powerful, but only available once you have significant scale.

Switching Costs — The product becomes sufficiently embedded in customers' workflows or lives that switching to a competitor is costly. Three types: Financial (direct monetary cost to switch), Procedural (time and effort to learn a new system), and Relational (loss of accumulated relationships, data, or status). Increase switching costs by introducing complementary products and services.

At Stability (mature stage), two final powers become available:

Brand — Creates either a "feel good" association or a "reduction in uncertainty" that enables pricing power. Brand is a late-stage power because it takes years of consistent behavior to build.

Process Power — The development over time (with many, many decisions throughout the organization) of company processes that both confer significant benefits and, due to their intrinsic complexity, cannot be easily emulated. This is not just operational excellence — it is a complex, evolved system that would take a competitor years to replicate even if they understood it conceptually.

Transformative Technology and the Innovation Process

Every generation gets a technology that rewrites the cost structure of creation. The printing press did it for knowledge distribution. The internet did it for communication. AI is the current candidate. The specific technology changes. The principles for how it reshapes innovation do not.

AI is the latest instantiation of a pattern that recurs whenever a transformative technology reaches sufficient maturity: what is expensive becomes cheap, what is scarce becomes abundant, and the bottlenecks migrate to places most teams are not looking. Four principles govern how we think about this shift.

Principle 1: When the cost of building drops by an order of magnitude, the bottleneck shifts from execution to judgment. For most of startup history, the hard part was building the thing. Engineering time was the binding constraint. That constraint is loosening. When prototyping costs collapse, the limiting factor is no longer "can we build it?" but "should we build it?" and "is this the right version to build?" Taste, conviction, and the willingness to kill your darlings become the scarce resources. Execution without judgment produces a lot of mediocre software very quickly.

Principle 2: When learning loops compress, the advantage goes to whoever defines the sharpest hypotheses. Transformative technology compresses the iteration cycle for everyone, which means raw speed becomes less differentiating. The new advantage is the quality of what you feed into each loop. A precise hypothesis tested in a fast cycle generates signal. A vague hypothesis tested in a fast cycle generates noise — faster.

Principle 3: When a technology is simultaneously a tool for executing the process AND a wave to be surfed, every step must be evaluated twice. This is the structural challenge that recurs with every general-purpose technology, and it is the one teams most often get wrong. The innovation process must be run through two lenses: "How does this technology change the way we execute this step?" and "Is this technology itself the wave we should be riding?" Conflating the two leads to building the wrong thing efficiently. Ignoring one leads to building the right thing with yesterday's tools.

Principle 4: When capabilities democratize, defensibility must come from assets that cannot be replicated by tools alone. Every transformative technology democratizes something that used to be gated. When everyone has access to the same capabilities, those capabilities stop being a moat. Defensibility migrates to what the tools cannot provide: exclusive relationships, network effects, earned trust, and the compounding knowledge that comes from running the innovation cycle hundreds of times. The 7 Powers framework applies with greater force when tools are democratized — the question "what do we have that a well-funded competitor with the same tools cannot replicate?" has a smaller answer set.

A fifth principle, specific to speed: Democratized building tools compress the time between a good idea and a competing implementation. The window between finding product-market fit and needing a real moat has narrowed. Strategy cannot wait as long as it used to.

A mental model for adoption. We think about integrating transformative technology across five modes: (1) Assistance — making existing tasks more efficient; (2) Delegation — handing off execution and accelerating iteration; (3) Interface — transforming how users interact with products; (4) Integration — embedding the technology as a core part of the product's value proposition; (5) Composability — creating surface area for external intelligence to build on top. These are not sequential stages that every company must progress through — they are options, and the right combination depends on the product and the market. The question we ask ourselves constantly: How would we do this if we were starting the company tomorrow?

Part IX

The Culture That Makes It Work

Why Process Without Culture Produces Bureaucracy, and Culture Without Process Produces Chaos

Everything in this document — every framework, every filter, every stage gate — is inert without the culture to animate it. A process can tell you what to do. Culture determines whether you'll actually do it when it's uncomfortable.

Founders, Not Functions

At the early stage, titles don't matter. Speed is every startup's only unfair advantage. To maintain that speed, every role — from marketing to community to product management — needs to engage in what Paul Graham, in his September 2024 essay, called "Founder Mode."

The distinction:

  • Manager Mode says: "What does my role require me to do?"
  • Founder Mode says: "What does the problem require me to do?"

New products fail when manager mode smothers founder mode. This doesn't mean hierarchy is bad or that managers are useless. It means that at the zero-to-one stage, everyone must operate as if the entire company's success depends on their personal decisions — because it does.

Bold Failure > Timid Success

We celebrate big swings and learning how to lose. This is not a platitude. It is a specific cultural commitment with specific behavioral implications:

  • When a Bold Beat fails, the team presents the learning, not excuses. The question is never "why did it fail?" — it's "what did we learn?"
  • When a safe bet succeeds modestly, we ask: "Did we learn anything we couldn't have predicted? Or did we just confirm what we already knew?" Confirmation is not learning.
  • The worst outcome is not failure. The worst outcome is ambiguity — shipping something too small to generate a clear signal.

The Scientific Method as Operating System

The scientific method diagram in our playbook is not a metaphor. It is literally how we operate: Observation/Question → Research Topic Area → Hypothesis → Test with Experiment → Analyze Data → Report Conclusions → (repeat).

Every ship is an experiment. Every experiment has a hypothesis. Every hypothesis is falsifiable. Every result — positive or negative — is data.

How Would We Do This If We Were Starting Tomorrow?

This is the most important question we can ask ourselves, and it should be asked constantly. The moment you stop asking it, you've calcified. The process described in this document is a snapshot, not scripture. It must evolve.

Epilogue: What This Document Doesn't Cover (Yet)

This document codifies the innovation process — how to go from zero to one. It is deliberately silent on what happens next.

By 2022, Dapper was simultaneously launching products with the NFL, the UFC, and La Liga in Europe. Disney Pinnacle followed in late 2023. An English Premier League product was built and never launched. The company grew from a focused team to over 600 employees, then contracted through three rounds of layoffs back to roughly 200.

That arc — the explosive scaling, the simultaneous multi-product launches, the hiring sprint, the market reversal, the contraction, the hard lessons about when to expand and when to hold — is a different story. It is the story of going from one to ten: how to scale in the right way following market signals, and what happens when the market moves faster than anyone imagined.

The process described in this document produced the ideas that powered that growth. A companion document will address the equally hard question of what to do when those ideas work — how to scale without destroying the thing that made them work in the first place.

Appendix B: Extended Case Studies

B.1: Microsoft Solitaire — The Frame That Taught the World to Click

In 1990, Microsoft included Solitaire with Windows 3.0. The common assumption is that it was entertainment. The reality is that it was a training tool. The mouse — pointing, clicking, and especially dragging — was a new and intimidating input method for users accustomed to keyboard-only interfaces. Solitaire made dragging and dropping intuitive and fun. It taught millions of users the fundamental interaction paradigm of graphical user interfaces without them ever knowing they were being taught.

This is the essence of a Frame: it constrains the infinite possibility of a new platform into a digestible behavior. The user thinks they're playing a game. They're actually learning the physics of the system.

B.2: Amazon Books — The Beachhead That Became an Empire

Jeff Bezos's selection of books as Amazon's first product category was not random. Books had specific properties that made them ideal for an online Beachhead: they were durable (no spoilage), universally catalogued (ISBNs), had virtually infinite SKU variety (no physical store could stock them all), and had a passionate customer base comfortable with mail-order (book clubs had existed for decades).

By dominating this niche, Bezos solved two problems simultaneously: he proved the e-commerce model worked (customer acquisition, fulfillment, returns), and he built the infrastructure — warehouses, logistics, customer data systems — that would power the expansion into every other product category. The Beachhead funded the invasion.

Appendix D: Annotated Further Reading List

For each referenced book or author, what a team member should read and why, where Roham's approach aligns or diverges, and the recommended priority.

D.1: 7 Powers — Hamilton Helmer

Priority: Essential. Read the entire book.

Helmer's framework is the backbone of how we think about strategy. The book is short (under 200 pages) and every chapter matters. Pay special attention to Chapter 1 (Strategy Statics): the foundational argument that strategy = power, and that without power, P=MC; Chapter 4 (Counter-Positioning): the most subtle power and the most commonly misunderstood; and Chapter 9 (The Power Progression): understanding when each power becomes available is as important as understanding what they are.

Where we align: Completely. The 7 Powers framework is used verbatim as both a culling filter (pre-PMF) and a strategic planning tool (post-PMF).

Where we adapt: Helmer discusses powers primarily in the context of established businesses. We apply the framework earlier — at the idea evaluation stage — as a forward-looking filter: "Can this idea plausibly build one or more powers?" This is a more speculative application than Helmer explicitly endorses, but we find it valuable.

D.2: The Lean Startup — Eric Ries

Priority: Essential for anyone in the RATs or Clock Speed phases.

Focus on Chapters 5-7 (Build-Measure-Learn): the core loop; Chapter 8 (Pivot or Persevere): the decision framework for when to change direction; and Chapter 9 (Innovation Accounting): the least-read and most important chapter.

Where we align: The Build-Measure-Learn loop maps directly to our Learning Loop (Measure → Feedback → Modify → Do/Redo).

Where we diverge: Ries is less prescriptive about which assumptions to test first. Our RATs framework — test the riskiest assumption first — is a more aggressive application of the same principle. Ries also doesn't distinguish between types of builds (our 6 archetypes); the Lean Startup treats every early-stage build as an MVP, which can lead to misclassifying Petri Dishes and Probes.

D.3: Crossing the Chasm — Geoffrey Moore

Priority: Essential for anyone working on go-to-market for a new product.

Focus on Part I (Discovering the Chasm): the Technology Adoption Lifecycle and why the gap between early adopters and early majority is lethal; and Part II (Crossing the Chasm): the D-Day analogy, the Bowling Alley strategy, and the "whole product" concept.

Where we align: The Beachhead archetype is a direct application of Moore's Bowling Alley strategy.

Where we adapt: Moore wrote primarily about B2B technology products. Our application to consumer products and crypto requires additional consideration of hype dynamics — the "Echo Chamber of Hype" failure mode that can make speculative adoption look like mainstream adoption.

D.4: The Innovator's Dilemma — Clayton Christensen

Priority: Essential. Chapters 1-4 are mandatory.

Focus on Chapters 1-2 (the foundational case studies) and Chapter 4 (the distinction between sustaining and disruptive innovation).

Where we align: We use the Innovator's Dilemma both as a culling filter and as an opportunity identifier.

Where we diverge: Christensen's later work on Jobs-to-be-Done is more directly applicable to our structured pitch format than the original disruption theory.

D.5: Six Thinking Hats — Edward de Bono

Priority: Useful for anyone facilitating brainstorms.

A quick read. Our adapted sequence (Blue → White → Red → Green → Yellow → Black) differs from de Bono's original; we front-load process definition and end with critical analysis because premature Black Hat thinking kills more ideas than it saves.

D.6: Founder Mode — Paul Graham (Essay, September 2024)

Priority: Essential for every team member.

Graham argues that the conventional wisdom about scaling companies — hire professional managers, delegate, create processes — can be fatal to innovation. The founder's direct involvement in product and technical decisions is not a bug to be fixed; it is a feature to be preserved. This maps directly to our "Founders, Not Functions" principle.
