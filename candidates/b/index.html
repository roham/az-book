How to farm lightning.

Innovation isn't random. It's an apparatus — and someone has to stay up and build it.

§ 001  /  Prelude

The myth is that great companies begin with a bolt from the sky. A founder in a shower. A napkin in a diner. A moment of grace that visits the deserving and skips the rest.

I have built companies for fifteen years and I have never seen it work that way. Not once.

What I have seen is this: a small number of operators who treat creativity the way a scientist treats a hypothesis, and a much larger number who treat it the way a gambler treats a hand. The first group compounds. The second group tells stories about the one time it almost worked. The difference is not taste. The difference is not intelligence. The difference is apparatus.

An apparatus is the unglamorous machinery that sits between an idea and a product — the intake, the triage, the gates, the kill criteria, the small ruthless meetings at 11pm where a thing you loved last Tuesday gets retired on Thursday because the signal did not show up. It is the sieve. It is the nine steps. It is the waitlist that taught you the thing you could not have learned from a deck. It is the Alpha that was allowed to be ugly because ugliness was the point. It is the willingness to farm luck instead of pray for it.

This book — these two books — are what I wish someone had handed me in 2011. Volume I is the thesis: nine steps, six archetypes, three portfolio tiers, one framework for saying yes and a harder one for saying no. Volume II is the apparatus: the phase-gate playbook that turned the thesis into a machine — waitlist to Alpha to Beta to GA, the signals that matter, the ones that lie, and the specific discipline of hound-dog hunting in the dark.

If you stay, you will learn how to build the thing that catches the lightning.

You will not learn how to make it strike.

Nobody has ever learned that.
