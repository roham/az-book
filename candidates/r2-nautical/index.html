FIRST ENTRY · SHIP'S LOG

I have spent twenty years trying to answer one question: can you farm innovation, or do you just get lucky? I now think the answer is both, and that the "both" is the whole craft. Lightning is real. It also lands more often on a prepared field. This book, in two volumes, is the field.

Volume I is the thesis. It is what I actually believe about building companies. There are nine steps that turn an idea into a product and a product into a business, and none of them are optional, and several of them are unglamorous. There are six archetypes — the people who reliably make things happen inside a studio, and the ones who confuse motion for progress, and how to tell the difference in a 45-minute meeting. There are three portfolio tiers: Sandbox Experiments & Research where the cost of being wrong is small, Big Bet Projects where the cost is enormous and the discipline must be proportional, and Established Business Units that have earned the right to compound. There is a Green Light Framework for deciding what to fund, and a culture section that I wrote for myself before I wrote it for anyone else. Volume I answers: what am I building, with whom, and why.

The thesis without the apparatus is a pitch deck. The apparatus without the thesis is a dashboard.

Volume II is the apparatus. It is the instrument you strap to the thesis so that reality can talk back to you on a clock. A waitlist is not a marketing tactic; it is an intake valve. An alpha is not a soft launch; it is a calibrated measurement where retention, activation, and signal density decide whether the next gate opens. A beta is not a bigger alpha; it is a different instrument entirely, tuned for cohort economics and hound-dog signal mining in the places users actually live. General availability is not a finish line; it is the moment the instrument hands the work to an operating unit that knows how to compound it. Volume II answers: how do I run the experiment, read the result, and know — on evidence, not vibes — when to promote.

Neither volume works alone. The thesis without the apparatus is a pitch deck. The apparatus without the thesis is a dashboard. Together they are a bench — the one I actually work at. This is that bench, open, for anyone who wants to sit down.
